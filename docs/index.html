Introduction

Choosing a college major is an important decision because it helps define what your future career will be. However, deciding what to major in can be very difficult because there are so many choices (there are approximately 175 majors listed on this page alone). Choosing a major becomes even more difficult when you want to take into account not only what your interests are, but also other factors, such as pay for example. This webpage aims to make it easier for you to consider those “other factors” when selecting a major by allowing you to explore various visualizations. In each section, the factors you can choose to compare majors by are median pay, unemployment rate, percentage of men vs. women, and percentage of jobs that are full time vs. part time. The data for all of the sections below is from https://github.com/fivethirtyeight/data/tree/master/college-majors. Also, in each visualization on this page, you can hover over the elements in the visualization (a dot in the scatterplot or a bar in the bar charts) to view the exact value being displayed. Note that for the bar charts, if you do not see a bar for a major or category, the value for that factor and major or category is zero. Also, this website has the best viewing experience when using Chrome (you may see unexpected behavior when using other browsers).

An Initial Exploration

To start you off on your search for a college major, explore the scatterplot below. All 175 majors are shown in the scatterplot and you can use the selectors to change which factor is displayed on the x and y axes. You can also hover over a point to see which major it corresponds to. The color of each point corresponds to the category of major it belongs to. As you explore the scatterplot, think about which factors are most important to you and which majors as well as categories of majors could be of interest to you based on those factors.

Examine Major Categories

Now that you have an idea of what factors are important to you, let’s take a closer look at the categories of majors. The chart below displays, for each category, the average value for the selected factor of the majors in that category. The selector below can be used to change which factor is displayed. In addition, you can click on a bar in the chart to see the top five majors for the factor you selected in the category that corresponds to the bar you clicked.

Dive into Majors

After examining the categories of majors and top five majors for various factors and categories, you may have an idea of which majors could be real possibilities for you. This section aims to help you compare those majors and hopefully narrow down the list of majors you’re considering. Click the select majors button below to open a modal where you can select up to 15 majors. To save your changes, whether you select majors or click deselect all, click the select button on the modal. Once you select at least one major, you will see a bar chart below, displaying the values for the majors and factor you selected.

Narrow down the Options

At this point, hopefully you have a relatively small list of majors you are still considering, perhaps around five majors or so. The goal of this section is to allow you to easily compare those majors on all factors to get a better idea of which major is the best for you. Similar to the previous section, click the select majors button below to open a modal where you can select up to 5 majors. Again, to save your changes, even after clicking deselect all, click the select button on the modal. Once you select at least one major, multiple bar charts will be displayed, showing the majors you selected and all factors.

Down to One

You made it to the last section of this webpage! After exploring all those visualizations in the previous sections, we hope you have decided on a major. If you have, then you can see information about that major by selecting the major in the first drop-down box below and clicking the “Go” button next to it. If not, no worries, we have options for that too. If you’re interested in a particular factor, such as finding the major with the highest median pay for example, you can use the second set of drop-down options to select a factor and whether you want to see the major with the lowest or highest value for that factor, then click the “Go” button next to it. If you’re tired of looking at majors and still aren’t sure which one to choose, then try clicking the “Random Major” button and see which major pops up.
